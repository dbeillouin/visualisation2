What is an Effect Size?

An effect size is a metric that quantifies the direction and magnitude of an experimental or observational effect. It is essential in meta-analysis as it standardizes results across different studies.

  • Extracted directly from publications or calculated from reported data.
  • Standardized to be comparable across multiple primary studies.
  • Reliable: Effect sizes should accurately represent the underlying data (e.g., caution is needed when using ratios with low denominators).

Effect sizes come in various forms depending on the type of data and research question (e.g., standardized mean difference, odds ratios, correlation coefficients).

Effect Size and p-value

When interpreting results, it is crucial to distinguish between statistical significance and practical significance.

  • Statistical vs. Practical Significance: Small effect sizes can produce significant p-values with large samples but may have little real-world impact. The focus should be on the magnitude of the effect rather than solely on the p-value.

  • Impact of Sample Size: Larger datasets can make even tiny effects statistically significant, leading to potential misinterpretation. This is why effect sizes are necessary to assess the real-world relevance of findings.

  • Criticisms of p-values: The p-value is often criticized for being overly sensitive to sample size and failing to convey the practical significance of an effect. Consider supplementing p-values with confidence intervals or Bayesian approaches for a more comprehensive interpretation.

For more on the limitations of p-values, see:

  • Chen et al., 2023

Conclusion

Effect sizes are essential metrics for understanding the practical relevance of study results. While p-values provide information about statistical significance, they do not capture the magnitude or direction of an effect. By combining effect sizes and p-values, researchers can gain a more nuanced understanding of study findings.

True vs. Estimated Effect Size

Effect sizes are typically represented by the Greek letter θ (theta):

  • θₖ: the ‘true’ (unknown) effect size of study k
  • \(\hat{θₖ}\): the observed (estimated) effect size from the sample data of study k

Steps to Calculate and Combine Effect Sizes

Step 1: Calculate Effect Sizes

  1. Identify the effect sizes to be calculated (e.g., Cohen’s d, Hedges’ g).
  2. Extract or calculate the mean, standard deviation, and sample size for each group.
  3. Use appropriate formulas depending on the effect size type.

Formula for Cohen’s d

\[ d = \frac{M_1 - M_2}{SD_{pooled}} \]

where: - \(M_1\) and \(M_2\) are the means of two groups. - \(SD_{pooled}\) is the pooled standard deviation:
\[ SD_{pooled} = \sqrt{\frac{(n_1 - 1) \cdot SD_1^2 + (n_2 - 1) \cdot SD_2^2}{n_1 + n_2 - 2}} \]

Formula for Hedges’ g

Hedges’ g corrects for small sample sizes using the formula:

\[ g = d \times \left( 1 - \frac{3}{4 \cdot (n_1 + n_2) - 9} \right) \]

Log Response Ratio (LRR)

\[ LRR = \log \left( \frac{M_{treatment}}{M_{control}} \right) \]

where \(M_{treatment}\) and \(M_{control}\) are the means of the treatment and control groups, respectively.

Step 2: Calculate the Variance of Each Effect Size

  • Variance for Cohen’s d:
    \[ Var(d) = \frac{n_1 + n_2}{n_1 \cdot n_2} + \frac{d^2}{2 \cdot (n_1 + n_2)} \]

  • Variance for Hedges’ g is calculated similarly to Cohen’s d.

  • Variance for Log Response Ratio (LRR):
    \[ Var(LRR) = \frac{SD_{treatment}^2}{n_{treatment} \cdot M_{treatment}^2} + \frac{SD_{control}^2}{n_{control} \cdot M_{control}^2} \]

Step 3: Combine Effect Sizes Using Meta-Analysis

Fixed-Effect Model

The combined effect size (θ) is calculated as:

\[ \hat{θ}_{FE} = \frac{\sum_{k=1}^{K} w_k \cdot \hat{θ}_k}{\sum_{k=1}^{K} w_k} \]

where: - \(w_k\) is the weight of each study, often defined as \(w_k = \frac{1}{Var(\hat{θ}_k)}\). - \(\hat{θ}_k\) is the observed effect size from each study.

Random-Effects Model

Random-effects models account for variability between studies using a between-study variance component (τ²):

\[ w_k = \frac{1}{Var(\hat{θ}_k) + τ²} \]

Then:

\[ \hat{θ}_{RE} = \frac{\sum_{k=1}^{K} w_k \cdot \hat{θ}_k}{\sum_{k=1}^{K} w_k} \]

Step 4: Calculate Confidence Intervals for Combined Effect Size

The standard error (SE) for the combined effect size is given by:

\[ SE(\hat{θ}) = \sqrt{\frac{1}{\sum_{k=1}^{K} w_k}} \]

The 95% confidence interval is then:

\[ CI_{95} = \hat{θ} \pm 1.96 \cdot SE(\hat{θ}) \]

Visualization Techniques

Forest Plots

Forest plots visually summarize the effect sizes of individual studies and the combined estimate.

  • Individual studies are represented as squares centered on the observed effect size.
  • The size of the square indicates the weight of each study.
  • Horizontal lines represent confidence intervals.
